First, we need to clarify a concept: a “round” in federated learning is not the same as an “epoch” in traditional machine learning. In federated learning, multiple clients are involved, and each client runs its own standard ML training process with its own epochs.

In federated learning, a “round” refers to the full process from distributing the model to clients, training locally, and then aggregating the models on the server. Within each round, every client runs its own epochs.

> 追问：你提到了Adam，能不能给解释一下？

Adam优化器的原理是同时维护着一个一阶矩和二阶矩。
其中一阶矩是对梯度进行滑动平均，可以理解为是”梯度的方向“，这在Adam的梯度更新函数中更加明显。

> Follow-up: You mentioned Adam—could you explain it?

ure.

The core idea of the Adam optimizer is that it maintains both a first moment and a second moment of the gradients.

- The **first moment** is the exponential moving average of the gradients, which can be understood as capturing the _direction_ of the gradient. This is clearly reflected in Adam’s update rule.
- The **second moment** is the exponential moving average of the squared gradients (i.e., the L2 norm), which can be interpreted as capturing the _magnitude_ (or length) of the gradient.

In contrast, traditional SGD updates parameters in a straightforward way:

$$\theta = \theta - \text{learning rate} \times \nabla \theta$$
Adam, however, replaces the raw gradient with the ratio of the first moment to the square root of the second moment.

This means:

- If the gradient’s L2 norm at a certain point is large (i.e., the gradient is “long”), it suggests that moving in that direction should be done more cautiously. As a result, the effective step size becomes smaller.
- If the L2 norm is small, the step size becomes relatively larger.

So Adam effectively adapts the step size dynamically based on the gradient statistics.

It’s worth noting that the “learning rate” here is not exactly the same as the traditional hyperparameter learning rate. Previously, the learning rate directly determined the step size. In Adam, it becomes a key factor that influences the step size, but the actual update is further modulated by the moment estimates.

As for a common variant, **AdamW**, it is quite straightforward: it adds weight decay to help prevent overfitting. In practice, this introduces an additional regularization term (with coefficient λ) into the parameter update.

Finally, in my federated fine-tuning project, the local training on each client follows a standard training pipeline, and we use the **AdamW optimizer** there.

所以压缩算法，我们实现了分布式学习最常见的算法，

> In federated fine-tuning, you mentioned compression algorithms. Could you explain them?

Sure.

Federated learning can be seen as a variant of distributed learning, so many techniques are shared between the two. Compression algorithms, in particular, originate from distributed learning and can be naturally applied to federated learning.

In our implementation, we adopted several commonly used compression methods:

**1. QSGD (Quantized SGD)**  
QSGD can compress 32-bit gradient data down to 8-bit or even 4-bit, while keeping the expectation of the gradients unchanged before and after quantization.

The idea is to normalize the gradient vector and map it into the range [0, 1], while preserving its scale using the L2 norm. During communication, the client only needs to transmit the sign of the gradient, the quantization level, and the normalization factor of the vector, which significantly reduces communication cost.

After quantization, the expectation remains the same, although the variance may change. However, this variance has a well-defined upper bound, which ensures good compression performance.

---

**2. Top-k Compression**  
This method is straightforward: it keeps only the top k% of gradient values with the largest magnitudes. In this way, the gradient becomes sparse, and only the most important updates are transmitted.

---

**3. PowerSGD**  
PowerSGD applies singular value decomposition (SVD) to the gradient matrix and retains only the most important k directions (i.e., low-rank approximation), thereby reducing the amount of data that needs to be communicated.

---

Overall, these compression methods effectively reduce communication overhead in federated learning while maintaining acceptable training performance.

> 你说你客户端在做的是传统的训练过程，完整讲解一下训练过程。

首先对微调数据做tokenizer，这里需要做两次分词
一次只对prompt，确定prompt的长度
一次对prompt和response都做分词，其中只有response部分参与后续的loss计算。
分词得到token以后，通过embedding变成向量序列，将向量序列经过一层层transformer block，每层中包含一次注意力计算和一次FFN，最后将生成的语句做loss计算
其中，LoRA主要在注意力模块起作用。本项目完全冻结了基座模型，然后在注意力的Q K V O矩阵，以及FFN的up down gate门控矩阵，都挂了LoRA低秩参数。

计算loss后自动求导，其实就是对注意力和FFN的每一个A和B去求梯度，求出梯度以后用AdamW进行优化。
以上就是一次完整的训练epoch，注意是epoch。在多个epoch结束之后，客户端开始做DP，做压缩然后上传服务器，客户端的一次训练就算完成了。

有时候，为了模拟隐私攻击，还会在训练的过程中做隐私攻击评估。隐私攻击评估指的是，当有一个攻击者拿到客户端上传的变化量时，他其实可以把梯度数据反过来推导出一条数据。
然后，根据推导得到的数据，攻击者开始猜测原样本数据，用原样本经模型生成梯度后，与自己窃取的梯度做余弦相似度匹配，来判断该模型中是否有某条样本数据参与训练。

> You mentioned that the client performs a standard training process. Could you walk through the full training pipeline?

Sure.

First, we tokenize the fine-tuning data. This involves two steps:

- The first tokenization is applied only to the prompt, in order to determine its length.
- The second tokenization is applied to both the prompt and the response. However, only the response part is used for loss computation.

After tokenization, the tokens are converted into vector representations through an embedding layer. The resulting sequence of vectors is then passed through multiple Transformer blocks. Each block consists of an attention module and a feed-forward network (FFN). Finally, the generated output is used to compute the loss.

LoRA mainly operates within the attention modules. In this project, the base model is completely frozen. We attach LoRA low-rank adapters to the Q, K, V, and O matrices in the attention layers, as well as to the up, down, and gate projection matrices in the FFN.

Once the loss is computed, gradients are obtained via automatic differentiation. In practice, this means computing gradients with respect to each pair of LoRA parameters (A and B) in both the attention and FFN modules. These parameters are then updated using the AdamW optimizer.

This completes one training epoch (note: epoch). After multiple epochs are finished, the client performs differential privacy (DP) processing, applies compression, and uploads the results to the server—this marks the completion of one round of local training on the client side.

In some cases, to simulate privacy attacks, we also perform privacy attack evaluations during training. This refers to scenarios where an attacker, after obtaining the updates uploaded by a client, attempts to reconstruct original data from the gradients.

Specifically, the attacker may try to reverse-engineer a data sample from the gradient information. Then, based on the reconstructed data, the attacker generates gradients from candidate original samples and compares them with the intercepted gradients using cosine similarity, in order to infer whether a particular data sample was involved in training.

> Could you explain different aggregation algorithms?

**FRLoRA:**  
On the server side, global matrices AAA and BBB are maintained. For each client, the low-rank update is computed in the form of a global BABABA plus a residual term.

---

**FLoRA:**  
Directly averaging the AAA and BBB matrices across clients is incorrect, as it introduces mathematical inconsistency and noise.  
Instead, the correct approach is to stack (concatenate) the clients’ AAA and BBB matrices to form larger global matrices AAA and BBB, which preserves mathematical correctness.

---

**TE-LoRA:**  
This method alternates freezing AAA and BBB during training—i.e., fixing one while updating the other.

DistriFusion 的核心流程是：在每个扩散时间步 ttt，先把输入特征 xtx_txt​ 按空间切成多个 patch 分给不同 GPU；每个 GPU 只计算自己那一块在各层 lll 的局部特征 At,(i)lA_{t,(i)}^lAt,(i)l​，同时把它“填回”上一时间步缓存的全局特征 At+1lA_{t+1}^lAt+1l​ 中，形成一个“旧+新”的近似完整特征图，然后仅对这块更新区域做卷积/注意力计算；与此同时，这些局部特征会通过异步 AllGather 在后台同步，供下一步复用为新的全局特征 AtlA_t^lAtl​。这样就利用“相邻时间步特征相似”的性质，用上一轮的全局信息替代实时通信，实现 patch 间交互，同时把通信隐藏在计算里，从而在保证图像质量的前提下实现多 GPU 并行加速。

> Explain DistriFusion in one paragraph

The core idea of DistriFusion is as follows: at each diffusion timestep $t$, the input feature $x_t$ is spatially divided into multiple patches and distributed across different GPUs.

Each GPU computes only the local features for its assigned patch at each layer $l$, denoted as $A_{t,(i)}^l$. These local features are then “filled back” into the cached global features from the previous timestep $A_{t+1}^l$, forming an approximate full feature map that combines “old + new” information. Computation (e.g., convolution or attention) is performed only on the updated regions.

Meanwhile, these local features are asynchronously synchronized across GPUs via AllGather in the background, so they can be reused as the updated global features $A_t^l$ in the next step.

By leveraging the similarity between adjacent timesteps, DistriFusion replaces real-time communication with cached global information, enabling interaction across patches while hiding communication behind computation. This achieves efficient multi-GPU parallel acceleration without sacrificing image quality.

> Explain U-Net

U-Net is a convolutional neural network with an encoder–decoder architecture. It works by “downsampling to extract semantic features” and “upsampling to recover spatial resolution,” while using skip connections to fuse low-level details with high-level semantics. This design allows it to maintain both global understanding and fine-grained local structure, which is especially useful for pixel-level tasks such as image segmentation and diffusion models.

During the downsampling stage, convolutional layers continuously extract features from the image. As the network goes deeper, the spatial resolution of feature maps decreases, while the number of channels increases.

After that, through linear layers—and in the case of diffusion models, additional self-attention and cross-attention modules—the model is able to better capture global information.

Group Normalization (GroupNorm) is used for feature normalization. Compared to Batch Normalization, it does not depend on batch size.

Finally, in the upsampling stage, the network reconstructs the spatial resolution of the image using the learned feature maps.

> What are the differences between U-Net and DiT?

In U-Net, attention is mainly used as a supplementary component, typically applied at lower resolutions. The core computation is still dominated by convolution. This is largely because DiT (Diffusion Transformer) has a significantly higher computational cost, and when dealing with high-resolution inputs, U-Net is often a more practical and efficient choice.

That said, U-Net is no longer the most active research direction. In contrast, DiT has recently gained a lot of attention, especially with the rise of AI video, and many optimization methods have been proposed to significantly improve its efficiency.

Moreover, from an industry perspective, attention-based architectures are more suitable for scaling laws, making them easier to scale up compared to convolution-based models.

> 你这里提到了ToCa算法，能讲讲这个ToCa算法吗

ToCa算法的核心在于，不同token的变化幅度不一样，因此需要针对性地缓存。

评分指标有4个：

1是self-attention的权重，表示”该token对其他token的贡献“。
2是对cross-attention求熵，表示对条件控制的影响力。比如有些图像token和文本token靠cross-attention相连，语义上更受文本条件约束的token，应该更频繁地刷新，而不是一直缓存。
3是缓存频率惩罚，如果token太久没有被更新，惩罚值就会增大。
4是空间均匀性，如果被缓存的token在空间上扎堆，集中在某个区域。那该区域的误差就会特别明显，因为你一整块都是误差啊。所以引入空间分布约束，在一个局部区域里，对最低分的token，提高他们的评分，防止缓存过于集中。

> You mentioned the ToCa algorithm—could you explain it?

The core idea of the ToCa algorithm is that different tokens exhibit different levels of change over time, so caching should be applied selectively rather than uniformly.

It uses four scoring criteria:

1. **Self-attention weights**  
    This measures how much a token contributes to other tokens. Tokens with higher influence are more important and should be updated more frequently.
2. **Entropy of cross-attention**  
    This reflects how strongly a token is affected by conditioning (e.g., text). For example, image tokens that are closely linked to text tokens via cross-attention—and thus more constrained by semantic conditions—should be refreshed more frequently rather than being cached for too long.
3. **Cache frequency penalty**  
    If a token has not been updated for a long time, its penalty increases, encouraging it to be refreshed.
4. **Spatial uniformity**  
    If cached tokens are spatially clustered in one region, the error in that region can become very noticeable. To address this, a spatial distribution constraint is introduced: within a local region, tokens with lower scores are boosted to prevent caching from becoming overly concentrated.

Overall, ToCa dynamically decides which tokens to cache or refresh based on these factors, improving efficiency while maintaining generation quality.
